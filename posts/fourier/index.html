Fourier Methods could be derived entirely from Group theory!#

As the title suggests, we could write down the whole episode of Fourier methods if we know some group theory. But why borther to derive a exisiting theory with another method. Well, Fourier methods are so important and common in our daily life. From the light we see and the sound we hear are a form of vibration that could break down into Fourier series. Those waves are the representation of a translational symmetry propagating with the plane wave with frequency proportion to the eigenvalues of the operator. This is one of the insight from Schur’s lemma, if a Hermitian operator commute with representation of the symmetry group, then \(H\) can be diagonalized into \(H = \lambda I\) where \(\lambda \) is the eigenvalues to the problem.

In the subsequent section, the results from representation theory will be breifly mentioned. And using these result, we derive the some of the theorem of Fourier method.

Representation theory: Honestly this is all we need#

Probably the most important theorem in group theory was the Schur’s lemma. It says that for a given operator \(H\) that commute with the representation of the group \(D(g)\) with each group member, the operator \(H\) will be diagonal with constant \(\lambda\) \(H = \lambda I\). Following these lemma, one could derive some powerful theorem for group representation which we will not go through the detail derivation. (For interested reader, see ( Citation: Zee, 2016 Zee, A. (2016). Group theory in a nutshell for physicists. Princeton University Press. Retrieved from https://books.google.co.jp/books?id=FWkujgEACAAJ ) )

$$ \sum_{g}{D^{(r)\dagger} (g)_ {j}^{i}D^{(s)}(g)_ {k}^{l}} = \frac{N(G)}{d}\delta_{l}^{i}\delta_{j}^{k} $$

It says that when sum over the representation \((r)\) and \((s)\) of the group, in most cases the summation vanishes except when

Taking the trace, we yield the following orthogonality relation.

  • Dimensions of the irreducible representations: $$ \sum_{r}{d_{r}^{2}} = N(G) $$

  • Irreducible representations are orthogonal when summed over class: $$ \sum_{c}{n_{c}(\chi^{(r)}(c))^{\ast} \chi^{(s)}(c)} = N(G) \delta ^{rs} $$

  • Classes of the group are orthogonal when summed over irreducible representation: $$ \sum_{r}{\chi^{(r)}(c)^{\ast} \chi^{(r)}(c^{\prime})} = \frac{N(G)}{n_{c}}\delta^{cc^{\prime}}$$

  • Number of irreducible representation equal to the number of class in the group

$$ N(C) = N(R) $$

  • A test on reducibility of representation \(r\)

$$ \sum_{c}{n_{c} \chi^{\ast(r)}\chi(c)} = N(G)n_{r} $$

Intuitively, how do we understand representation of the group. We note that group is defined how an element within the group multiply but without a way of representing these reltion its applicablity is limited. A representation preserves the multiplication structure of the group.

$$ D(g)D(g^{\prime}) = D(gg^{\prime}) $$

So, without any lost of generality, it will be easier to work with the representation of the group (such as matrices or tensors)instead of the abstract algebraic relation of the group multiplication.

Symmetry and $Z_{N}$#

Our first exposure to Fourier Transform or Fourier method is usually associating it with periodicity, a repetition pattern in time. However, a group does not differentiate the time with any arbitrary variable as long as they have the same symmetric pattern. Say for example, we partition rotation of \(2\pi\) into \( N \) parts. We could visualize it on the complex plane with each partition represented by \(e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}j}\) for \(j = 0, 1, …, N-1\). We recognize this is the \(Z_{N}\) group where \(g^{N} = I\). Since \(Z_{N}\) is an abelian group, then each element is a class of itself hence by the fact that the number of irreducible representation equals to the number of classes \(N(R)=N(C)\) each with dimension of \(d_{r} = 1\) by equation \(\sum_{N}d_{r}^{2} = N\).

Let’s take an educated guess of the irreducible representation. To satisfy requirement $$ D(e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}j})D(e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}j^{\prime}}) = D(e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}(j+j^{\prime})}) $$

the representation need to be an multiplication factor in the exponent to preseve the multiplication structure. Hence, we propose

$$ D(e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}j}) = e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk} \quad k = 0, 1, …, N-1$$

One could check that the multiplication structure indeed does preserved.

Now we finally equipped to unleash the power of group theory. The orthogonality of both class and irreducible representation is saying that

$$ \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=0}^{N}{e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk} e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk^{\prime}}} = \delta^{kk^{\prime}} $$

$$ \sum_{k=0}^{N}{e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk} e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}j^{\prime}}} = \delta^{jj^{\prime}} $$

This is the same old discrete fourier transform and inverse discrete fourier transform but entirely from group theory along.

Okay, but how about the idea that for a given function that compose of different vibration mode. Recall that we could break down that function into weighted component of fourier parts. What do group theoretic approach has to do with this?

We recognize this given function as an reducible representation that contains various irreducible representation \(n_{r}\) times.

So, given the reducible representation \(\chi(j)\) applying the test of reducibility gives the weight of the fourier component \( n_{k} \)

$$ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk} \chi(j)}= n_{k} $$

Then, the reducible representation can be written as the sum of each of irreducible representation with weight \( n_{k} \)

$$ \chi(j) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{n_{k}e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk}} $$

Also known as the Fourier series.

Continum limit: from $Z_{N}$ to $U(1)$ and from DFT to FT#

Jumping from the discrete case to the continuous case require we move to the limit \(N \rightarrow \infty\) and the summation becomes \(\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \rightarrow \int_{0}^{\infty}d\mu(g)\), where \(\mu(g)\) is the group measure.

Now there are two options to account for the continuous case. One being recognizing in the continuous limit the discrete group \(Z_{N}\) becomes \(U(1)\). Starting from here, we could use the methodology of continuous group to derive several orthogonality theorem. Another one being we start from our result of discrete case and take the continuous limit. Obviously, the second method will be much more straight foward but we will instead take a detour on the first method to explore more aspects on group theoretic approach.

  • find the trace

\(U(1)\) group has the property that \(U^{\dagger}U = I\). For \(U(1)\), it furnishes one dimension representation only and hence the trace of the representation is just the representation itself. We propose that \(D(\theta) = \chi(\theta, k) = e^{i\theta k}\). It clearly satisfies the unitary condition and preserve the algebraic structure of the group multiplication. The only difference is that now \(\theta\) runs as continuous variable from \(0 < \theta <2\pi\).

  • find the group measure

The purpose of finding a group measure is to make the integral cover the group manifold. Hence, we could intepret the group measure \(d\mu(g)\) runs over the group manifold. In this trivial case of \(U(1)\), the group manifold is merely a cycle and hence to cover the manifold we propose the following integral \(\int_{0}^{2\pi}{d\theta}\).

Applying the orthogonality theorem in the discrete case we write

$$ \int_{U(1)}{\chi^{\ast}(k,g)\chi(k^{\prime},g)d\mu(g)} = \int_{0}^{2\pi}{e^{-i\theta k^{\prime}}e^{i\theta k}d\theta} = 2\pi \delta_{kk^{\prime}}$$

Final Remark#

To conclude, from the group theoretic approach, we recover the following relation

Result from Discrete Group

  • DFT

$$ \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=0}^{N}{e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk} e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk^{\prime}}} = \delta^{kk^{\prime}} $$

  • Inverse DFT

$$ \sum_{k=0}^{N}{e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk} e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}j^{\prime}}} = \delta^{jj^{\prime}} $$

  • Fourier Series

$$ \chi(j) = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1}{n_{k}e^{i\frac{2\pi}{N}jk}} $$

Result from Continuous Group

  • Fourier Transform

$$ \int_{U(1)}{\chi^{\ast}(k,g)\chi(k^{\prime},g)d\mu(g)} = \int_{0}^{2\pi}{e^{-i\theta k^{\prime}}e^{i\theta k}d\theta} = 2\pi \delta_{kk^{\prime}}$$
