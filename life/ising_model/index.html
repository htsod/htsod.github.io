This blog was motivated by the wonder of why there is a phase transition in Ising model in two-dimension and higher dimension but not in one dimension. For a historical review, Ising initial work on lattice model to solve for the phase transition in magnet was a dissapointment when he evaluated the 1D Ising model analytically. The one dimension Ising model predicts no phase transition for magnet in contrast to the experimental observation. However, when the dimension gets to 2 or higher, numerical solution shows abrupt change of material’s properties suggesting a possible phase transition of magnetism of metals at a sufficient low temperature.

The interaction rules for the one-dimension and two-dimension Ising model are the same. The Hamiltonian involves the interaction from the closest neighbor and an external agent.

$$ H = -2J \sum_{i>j}\sigma_{i} \sigma_{j} - \mu B \sum_{i} \sigma_{i} $$

Whereas the computation result has shown an entirely different result. That suggest dimensionality is playing a role

Ising model is an lattice model that captures the evolution of states influenced by their neighbor states and an external agent. It was introduced to tackle the problem of phase transition of ferromagnetism but later adopted into other fields of science. It could be applied to phase transition problem with two distincitve states and the evolving probability can be calculated from the current state. In this review of Ising model, I will show the steps of solving the two dimensional Ising Model with a modified Monte Carlo Scheme proposed in ( Citation: Ashkan Shekaari, 2021 Ashkan Shekaari, M. (2021). Theory and simulation of the ising model. ) .

Introduction#

For subatomic system, all interactions have to be quantum mechanical. So in deriving the Hamiltonian of the Ising Model, we need to take the coupling of the orbital and spin angular momentum into account to derive the Hamiltonian in the system.

In the case of ferromagnetic materials, it turn out that from theoretical and experimental investigations have so far shown that, for all ferromagnetic materials, data on the temperatuer depenednece of M fit best with the values of total angular momentum of \( J = 1/2 \) and gyroscopic ratio of \( g = 2.0023\approx 2 \) in this model. ( Citation: Ashkan Shekaari, 2021 Ashkan Shekaari, M. (2021). Theory and simulation of the ising model. ) So, we could consider only the spin degree of freedom matters. It is also reasonable that the spin interaction is short range, meaning only the nearest neighboring atoms matters to the evolution of the states of a chosen atom.

Writing out the one dimensionall Ising Hamiltonian

$$ H = -2J \sum_{i>j}\sigma_{i} \sigma_{j} - \mu B \sum_{i} \sigma_{i} $$

Where the \( \sigma \) representing the spin of atom \( i,j \), and it could be positive 1 or negative 1 to denote the top spin and down spin. The first summation include all the nearest neighbor interaction where J is the interaction constant. While the second summation shows the total interaction from a external magnetic field of \(B\) with a magnetic constant of \( \mu \).

The transfer matrix method aims to solve for the thermodynamic properties of the system by calculating the partition function from the Hamiltonian. One could treat the partition function as equal share of the system in terms of parameters such as temperature of the system. Then, observable quantities that are based on the system’s average becomes calculatable such as the free energy of the system as well as its physical properties in terms of system’s parameters.

Transfer matrix method#

Writting the Hamiltonian operator in energy and re-define the constant for simplification we get the following expression

$$ E(\sigma) = -J \sum_{j}^{N} \sigma_{j} \sigma_{j+1} - H \sum_{j}^{N} \sigma_{j} $$

With the partition function defined to be \( Z = e^{\beta E} \) , where \( \beta = k_{b}T \) and \( k_{b} \) is the Boltzemann constant and \(T\) the temperature of the system. The partition function for the one dimension Ising Model is then written as

$$ Z_{N} = \sum_{\sigma} exp \left[ k\sum_{j}^{N} \sigma_{j} \sigma_{j+1} + h \sum_{j}^{N} \sigma_{j} \right] $$

By some arithmatic derivation which we will not be going through here, the N partition function \( Z_{N} \) could be written as the trace of a N matrix product \( T^{N} \), \( Z_{N} = tr(T^{N}) \) where T is defined to be the transfer matrix and is given by

$$ V = [[e^{k+h}, e^{-k}], [e^{-k}, e^{k-h}]] $$

It turns out that the transfer matrix is symmetric hence could be diagonlized into matrix with entries of its eigenvalues. As shown in the follwing equations

$$ T = PDP^{-1} = P[[\lambda_{1}, 0], [0, \lambda_{2}]]P^{-1} $$

Carrying out N multiplication,

$$ T^{N} = PDP^{-1}PDP^{-1} … PDP^{-1}= PD^{N}P^{-1} $$

Using the property of trace that the trace of a matrix would be invariant under similarity transformation, the partition function is further simplify to summation of the eigenvalues of the transfer matrix

$$ Z_{N} = tr(PD^{N}P^{-1}) = tr(D^{N}) = \lambda_{+}^{N} + \lambda_{-}^{N} $$

In the language of thermodynamic, the partition function has the physical significant of statistical portion of a system. With all the states equally probable then the partion function of N particles captures all the macroscopic properties such as the free energy which is defined to be \( F = \ln{Z_{N}} \).

Introducing the quantity free energy per lattice site \( f = F/N \), we will be looking at a expression that is independet to the size of the system.

$$ \beta N^{-1}F = \ln(\lambda_{1}) + N^{-1} \ln \left[ 1+ \left( \frac{\lambda_{2}}{\lambda_{1}} \right)^{N} \right]$$

Assuming \( \lambda_{1} > \lambda_{2} \), and in the thermodynamic limit we expect \( N \rightarrow \infty \) then the last term vanish and giving us the free energy per lattice site as

$$ f = -k_{B}T \ln(\lambda_{1}) $$

Solving \( \lambda \) from the transfer matrix, which yields

$$ \lambda_{\pm} = e^{k}\cosh{h} \pm (e^{2k}\sinh^{2}h + e^{-2k})^{1/2} $$

Subsequently the free energy per site could be written as

$$ f(h, T) = -k_{B}T \ln{[e^{k} \cosh{h} + (e^{2k}\sinh^{2}h + e^{-2k})^{1/2}]}$$

And the magnetization which is defined to be \( \frac{\delta f(h, T)}{\delta h}\) could be written as

$$ \frac{\delta f(h, T)}{\delta h} = M(h, T) = \frac{e^{k}\sinh{\beta H}}{(e^{2k}\sinh^{2}\beta H + e^{-2k})^{1/2}}$$

We visualize the dependence of magnetization on the external field with three different temperature values.

As shown in the above figure, in the lowest temperature where \(\beta = 1.5 \), the spin align perfectly to the external field. As the temperature goes up, the spin orientation becomes less susceptible to the external field.

Noting that \( M(H, T) \) being an analytic function for real \( H \) and non-zero positive \( T \). Meaning the physical and observable quantity in the system undergoes smooth transition with varing external magnetic field or change of temperature. Hence, there will be no phase transitions for the one dimensional Ising model.

Introduction#

Recall the Hamiltonian of the one dimensional Ising model is written as \( H = -2J \sum_{i>j}\sigma_{i} \sigma_{j} - \mu B \sum_{i} \sigma_{i} \). Extending it to two dimension, we have to take row and column into account, which introduces an additional indice into the Hamiltonian.

$$ H = -J \sum_{(i,j)}\sigma_{i} \sigma_{j} -J^{\prime} \sum_{(i,k)}\sigma_{i} \sigma_{k} $$

With this in hand, the system could be broken down into canonical ensemble with a Boltzmann weight factor \( e^{-\beta E}(\beta = 1/k_{B}T) \) at the equilibrium temperature. The general procedure will then be evolve the state with probability governs by the Boltzmann weight factor for a particular temperature \( T \). Iterativelly carrying out this procedure bring the system to equilibrium state.

Then given an observable \( A \) in a D-dimensional lattice with N identical spins localized on the lattice points, the average of the termodynamic observable \( A \) pertaining to this system in say the canonical ensemble is then given by

$$ \left< A \right> = \frac{\left[ \int{A(q, p)e^{-\beta E(q, p)}d^{DN}q d^{DN}p} \right]}{\left[ \int{e^{-\beta E(q, p)}d^{DN}qd^{DN}p} \right]}$$

However, numerical solution to the above many dimensional integral is quite in possible as for \( N \approx 10^{23} \). Resorting to MC methods, the above equation accordinly turns into the simple unweighted average

$$ \left< A \right> = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} A(q_{i}) $$

where \( M \) is the number of microstates \( q_{i} \) within the sample {\( q \)} - a sample, here, is referred to as a set of microstates, which is in turn a subset of the ensemble.

Numerical Solution to 2D Ising Model#

The modified Monte Carlo scheme#

In equilibrium statistical mechanics, an MC simulation uses pseudorandom number generators to sample the ensemble of the system according to the equilibrium probability distribution

$$ P(q) = \frac{e^{\beta E(q)}}{\sum_{q^{\prime}} e^{-\beta E(q^{\prime})}} $$

where the summation is carried out over all members of the set {\( q^{\prime} \)}, an MC simulation smaples configurations (microstates) according to the Boltzmann weight factor \( e^{\beta E} \) and then weights them evenly, instead of drawing a smaple randomly and then weighting them by \( e^{-\beta E} \).

The Metropolis algorithm#

The Metropolis algorithm is a Markov chain Monte Carlo method for obtaining a sequence of random samples from a probability distributio from which direct sampling is difficult.

Random walk in the sampling space, i.e, selecting a random microstate q from the set {q} with a probability distribution approaching \( P(q) \), is accomplished by the Metropolis algorithm. Using \( P(q) \) for two consecutives states \( q\) and \( q^{\prime}\) (the system moves from the former to the latter state in its phase space), it is obtained that

$$ \frac{P(q^{\prime})}{P(q)} = e^{-\beta \left[ E(q^{\prime}) - E(q) \right]} = e^{-\prime \Delta E}$$

One important feature of this ratio is that it depends only on the energy difference between any two states, therefore, there is no need to have energies of all the microstates as included in the denominator of \( \left< A \right> = \frac{\left[ \int{A(q, p)e^{-\beta E(q, p)}d^{DN}q d^{DN}p} \right]}{\left[ \int{e^{-\beta E(q, p)}d^{DN}qd^{DN}p} \right]} \).

The Metropolis algorithm is accomplished in the following five steps:

  • Step I. Change the present microstate \( q \) of the system to another randomly chosen state \( q^{\prime} \). In an Ising problem, this is equivalent to flipping a randomly selected spin. Since the total energy (E) explicitly appears in the Boltzmann weight factor, it must then be an explicit function of the microstate of the system in a way that change in q directly changes E.

  • Step II. Calculate the energy difference \( \Delta E = E(q^{\prime} = E(q)) \)

  • Step III. Perform steps I and II once for each spin in the system. The spins are usually selected randomly to ensure detailed balance. Steps I through III also define one MC sweep.

  • Step IV. Repeat steps I through III for a number (eqstp in the codes) of MC sweeps in order for the entire system to be equilibrated. This value cannot be indeed estimated a priori. As a result, for an arbitrary value of temperature in the temperature loop of the code, one has to plot the total energy of the system at each step of equilibration and then check whether convergence is achieved.

Ergodicity#

Abstractly, Ergodicity could be defined by following

Definition 1: In an ergodic system, the trajectory of almost every point in phase space enventually passses arbitrarily close to every other point (position and momentum) on the surface of constant energy.

Definition 2: A time evolution in a set S is ergodic if and only if all the ergodic components R in S either have zero volume or have a volume equal to the volume of S.

To ensure the algorithm’s ergodicity, or in other words, it approaches the equilibrium state. The following conditions need to be met.

Theorem A discrete dynamical system with a finite number of states can be guaranteed to converge to an equilibrium distribution \( \rho^{ast} \) if the computer algorithm

  • is Markovian (has no memory),
  • is ergodic (can reach everywhere and is acyclic), and
  • satisfies detailed balance

Result#

By running the algorithm with the following specification

L = 20         # Size of the square lattice
mcstp = 2000   # Number of MC sweep
eqstp = 2000   # Number of MC sweep for equilibration
D = 2          # Lattice dimension
N = L ** D     # Number of spins
NN = 4         # Number of nearest-neighbors
T_0 = 0.5      # Initial temperature
T_f = 10.0     # Final temperature
dT = 0.005     # Temperature step

We visualize four system properties as we vary the temperature in ratio of the critical temperature \( T_{c} \).

As shown in the graphs, all four properties demonstrated a sharp transition at \( T = T_{c} \). Clearly there is a phase transition at \( T_{c} \) for the two dimensional Ising model. At critical temperature, the material losses its magnetization and the correlation length drops to zero. To take in more energy to sustain the phase transition, the specific heat also raises at the critical temperature.

Final Remarks#

As claimed ealier in this blog and what we have shown, there is no phase transition in the one dimensional Ising Model. In fact, Ising, being first one to solve the model further concluded that there will be no phase transition in arbitrary dimension. However, we known that it is not true seeing the extensive application of Ising Model of higher dimensional space in various field of science.
